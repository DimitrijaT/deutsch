1. Sentence Structure (Satzbau)

Understanding where to place the verb is the most fundamental rule in German. Everything else in the sentence moves around the verb.

ðŸ“œ Rule 1: The Verb is in Position 2 (Statements)

In a standard declarative sentence, the conjugated verb always comes in the second position. The subject (who/what is doing the action) is usually in the first position.

Ich wohne in Berlin. (I live in Berlin.)

Wir lernen jeden Tag Deutsch. (We learn German every day.)

Mein Name ist Max. (My name is Max.)

ðŸ“œ Rule 2: The Verb is in Position 1 (Yes/No Questions)

To form a question that can be answered with "ja" or "nein", move the conjugated verb to the first position. The subject follows in the second position.

Wohnst du in Berlin? (Do you live in Berlin?)

Haben Sie Kinder? (Do you have children?)

Ist das dein Auto? (Is that your car?)

ðŸ“œ Rule 3: W-Questions (Information Questions)

For questions asking for information (Wer, Was, Wo, Wie, etc.), the question word (W-Wort) is in position 1, and the verb remains in position 2.

Wo wohnst du? (Where do you live?)

Wie heiÃŸen Sie? (What is your name?)

Was machen Sie beruflich? (What do you do for a living?)

Wann beginnt der Film? (When does the film begin?)

ðŸ“œ Rule 4: Inversion (When Position 1 is Not the Subject)

You can start a sentence with elements other than the subject (like time, place, or manner) to add emphasis. When you do this, the verb still stays in position 2, and the subject moves to position 3. This is called inversion.

Heute lerne ich Deutsch. (Today, I am learning German.)

Am Montag arbeite ich nicht. (On Monday, I don't work.)

In Berlin gibt es viele Museen. (In Berlin, there are many museums.)

ðŸ“œ Separable Verbs (Trennbare Verben)

Many German verbs have a prefix that separates and moves to the very end of the sentence. Common separable prefixes include: an-, auf-, aus-, ein-, mit-, ab-, zu-, vor-.

Ich rufe dich morgen an. (from anrufen - to call)

Der Zug kommt um 10 Uhr an. (from ankommen - to arrive)

Wann stehst du normalerweise auf? (from aufstehen - to get up)

Wir kaufen im Supermarkt ein. (from einkaufen - to shop)

With a modal verb, the separable verb stays together at the end (it is not separated).

Ich muss dich morgen anrufen.

Wir wollen heute einkaufen.

ðŸ“œ The Imperative (Commands)

The imperative mood is used to give commands, make requests, or offer advice. There are three main forms, depending on who you are speaking to.

Politeness and the Imperative

Choosing the correct form is crucial for politeness. Use Sie for formal situations or with strangers. Use du for one friend, family member, or child. Use ihr for a group of people you know well.

The Imperative and Cases (e.g., Accusative)

The imperative itself is a mood, not a case. The verb you use in your command determines which case is needed for the object. Many common command verbs (like "buy", "read", "take") require an accusative object.

Accusative: Kauf den Apfel! (The verb "kaufen" requires an accusative object.)

Accusative: Lies das Buch! (The verb "lesen" requires an accusative object.)

Dative (A2 topic): Hilf mir! (The verb "helfen" requires a dative object, not accusative.)

No Object: Schlaf gut! (The verb "schlafen" here requires no object.)
