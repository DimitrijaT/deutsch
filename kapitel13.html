Chapter 13: Familie und Freunde (Family and Friends)

This chapter explores family life, from everyday chores and communication to celebrating major milestones. We'll learn the modal verb sollen to talk about duties and requests, master the question words wer, wen, and wem, and use demonstrative articles to describe family photos.

Theme 1: Household Chores (Hausarbeit)

A survey in Germany shows what people enjoy—and don't enjoy—doing around the house. Cooking (kochen) is popular, but chores like ironing (bügeln), washing dishes (Geschirr spülen), and cleaning the bathroom (Bad putzen) are not.

Grammar Focus: The Modal Verb `sollen`

sollen is used to report a request, an order, or a duty given by someone else. It translates to "should" or "is supposed to." Like other modal verbs, it sends the main verb (in infinitive form) to the end of the sentence.

Personsollen (to be supposed to)
ich / er / sie / essoll
dusollst
wir / sie / Siesollen
ihrsollt
Papa sagt, ich soll die Spülmaschine ausräumen.
(Dad says I'm supposed to unload the dishwasher.)
Dialogue: Family Instructions
Sophie: Ich habe eine Nachricht von Papa. Ich soll für das Frühstück einkaufen und die Waschmaschine einschalten.
Timo: Ich auch. Ich soll Lina abholen und die Spülmaschine ausräumen.
Sophie: Und was essen wir?
Timo: Wir sollen Pizza bestellen. Super, oder?

Theme 2: Family History and Celebrations

Families celebrate many milestones together. In Germany, special names are given to major wedding anniversaries.

Wedding Anniversaries in Germany
  • 25 Years: die Silberhochzeit (Silver Wedding)
  • 50 Years: die Goldene Hochzeit (Golden Wedding)
  • 60 Years: die Diamanthochzeit (Diamond Wedding)

Talking About the Past

To talk about when something happened, we use specific time expressions.

Wann war das? (When was that?)
→ Das war vor 60 Jahren. (That was 60 years ago.)
→ Das ist lange her. (That was a long time ago.)
→ Das war Weihnachten 1995 (neunzehnhundertfünfundneunzig).

Grammar Focus 2: Question Words for People

Choosing the correct question word for "who" depends on the grammatical case—whether the person is the subject, direct object, or indirect object.

Wer? Wen? Wem?
CaseFunctionQuestion WordExample
NominativeSubject (Who is doing the action?)Wer?Wer ist das? (Who is that?)
AccusativeDirect Object (Who is being acted upon?)Wen?Wen kennst du hier? (Whom do you know here?)
DativeIndirect Object (To/with whom?)Wem?Mit wem telefonierst du? (With whom are you talking?)

Grammar Focus 3: Demonstrative Articles

Demonstrative articles ("this," "that," "these") are used to point to a specific person or thing. They have the same endings as definite articles (der, das, die).
