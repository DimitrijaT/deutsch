Chapter 3: Freunde und Co. (Friends & Co.)

In this chapter, you will learn more about leisure activities in Germany. You will explore various hobbies, learn how to talk about what you enjoy, and master the essentials of arranging to meet up with friends.

Kulturtipp (Culture Tip): Planning is Key Germans are known for their love of planning. While spontaneous meetups happen, it's very common to plan activities with friends days or even weeks in advance, especially for things like parties or weekend trips. Using phrases like "Hast du am Samstag Zeit?" (Do you have time on Saturday?) is a standard way to initiate plans.

1. Hobbies and Leisure Activities

Vocabulary: Hobbies (Hobbys)

Some hobbies in German are formed with a noun and a verb, while others are just a single verb.

TypeGerman ActivitiesEnglish Meaning
Noun + Verb Volleyball spielen, Basketball spielen, Ski fahren, Rad fahren (or Fahrrad fahren), Yoga machen, Musik hören to play volleyball, to play basketball, to go skiing, to ride a bike, to do yoga, to listen to music
Single Verb joggen, fotografieren, kochen, lesen, schwimmen, wandern to jog, to photograph, to cook, to read, to swim, to hike

Grammar: Using gern to Express Likes

To say you like doing something, you don't use a special verb like "to like." Instead, you add the adverb gern after the conjugated verb.

Key Grammar Rule: Position of gern gern follows the verb to express enjoyment. To say you don't like doing something, you use nicht gern.
Ich spiele gern Volleyball. (I like to play volleyball.)
Anna macht gern Yoga. (Anna likes to do yoga.)
Stefan fährt nicht gern Ski. (Stefan does not like to go skiing.)

2. Arranging to Meet (Sich verabreden)

Vocabulary: Days of the Week (Wochentage)

  • Montag (Monday)
  • Dienstag (Tuesday)
  • Mittwoch (Wednesday)
  • Donnerstag (Thursday)
  • Freitag (Friday)
  • Samstag (Saturday) - also called Sonnabend in some parts of Germany.
  • Sonntag (Sunday)

The weekend (das Wochenende) consists of Samstag and Sonntag.

Grammar: Prepositions of Time

To specify when something happens, you need the correct preposition. For days and times of day, you'll use am.

Time Prepositions: am and um
  • Use am for days of the week (am Montag) and times of the day (am Nachmittag). Exception: in der Nacht (in the night).
  • Use um for specific clock times (um 16 Uhr).

Reading Comprehension: Making Plans

Read these short messages where people are trying to make plans. Notice the use of time prepositions.

Message 1: "Hi Julia, wie geht’s? Lernen wir am Dienstag gemeinsam? Vielleicht am Abend? Hast du Zeit? LG"

Message 2: "Lieber Sascha, wie geht’s dir? Spielen wir am Mittwoch Tennis? Vielleicht am Nachmittag? Hast du Zeit? LG"

Message 3: "Hi Stefan, wie geht’s? Wir spielen Volleyball. Um 16 Uhr im Olympiapark. Hast du Zeit? LG, Julia"

Sprechtraining (Speaking Practice) Practice making plans with a partner based on the following dialogue.
A: Hi [Partner's Name], was machst du am Wochenende?
B: Ich fahre vielleicht Ski. Fährst du auch gern Ski?
A: Nein, ich fahre nicht gern Ski. Aber ich spiele gern Volleyball.
B: Super! Spielen wir am Samstag Volleyball? Vielleicht um 16 Uhr?
A: Ja, gute Idee! Ich habe Zeit.

3. Grammar: Indefinite and Negative Articles

In Chapter 2, you learned the definite articles (der, die, das). Now we focus on the indefinite ('a'/'an') and negative ('no'/'not a') articles.

Key Grammar Rule: ein vs. kein The negative article kein is used to negate nouns. It takes the exact same endings as the indefinite article ein. In the plural, where there is no indefinite article, you use keine.
GenderIndefinite Article ('a'/'an')Negative Article ('no')
Masculine (der)ein Rucksackkein Rucksack
Feminine (die)eine Taschekeine Tasche
Neuter (das)ein Buchkein Buch
Plural (die)- (Bücher)keine Bücher
A: Ist das ein Schlüssel? (Is that a key?)
B: Ja, das ist ein Schlüssel.

A: Ist das eine Geburtstagsparty? (Is that a birthday party?)
B: Nein, das ist keine Geburtstagsparty. Das ist eine Hochzeit (a wedding).
