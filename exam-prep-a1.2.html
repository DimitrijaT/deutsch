Written Exam (Schriftlicher Teil)

H√∂ren (Listening)

You will listen to short announcements and conversations and choose the correct answer. The key is to listen for specific information like times, places, names, and numbers.

Top Tips for Listening
  • Read the questions *before* you listen. This helps you know what information to focus on.
  • Don't panic if you don't understand every word. Focus on the main idea and the key words related to the question.
  • Pay attention to numbers, dates, times, and place names.

Lesen (Reading)

You will read short texts like emails, advertisements, or signs and answer questions (True/False or multiple choice).

Top Tips for Reading
  • Skim the text first to get a general idea of the topic.
  • Read the questions carefully, then find the part of the text that contains the answer.
  • Watch out for words like "nicht", "kein", "immer", or "nie" as they can completely change the meaning.

Schreiben (Writing) - Detailed Guide

The writing part of the exam has two tasks and takes about 20 minutes. You will first fill out a form based on given information, and then write a short, simple email. This section tests your ability to understand information and communicate basic needs in writing.

Task A: Fill out a Form (Formular ausf√ºllen)

In this task, you will read a short text containing personal details about someone. Your job is to accurately transfer five key pieces of information into a registration form. Pay close attention to details like names, ages, cities, and professions.

Top Tips for the Form
  • Read Carefully: Read the text two or three times to make sure you understand all the details.
  • Identify Key Information: Look for names, numbers, places, and jobs. Underline them if you can.
  • Spelling is Crucial: Copy names and places exactly as they are written in the text. A small spelling mistake can cost you a point.
  • Match Information to Field: Make sure the information you write matches the label on the form (e.g., put the "Familienname" in the correct box).

Task B: Write a Short E-Mail (Eine E-Mail schreiben)

This is the core of the writing test. You will be given a situation and three bullet points that you *must* include in your email. The email should be around 30 words. You can write more, but make sure it is correct! The structure (salutation, body, closing) is very important.

The Golden Rules of Email Writing
  • Address all three points: You MUST include information related to all three bullet points given in the task. This is the most important part.
  • Correct Salutation & Closing: Use the right formal or informal greeting and sign-off. Remember the comma after the salutation!
    • Formal: Sehr geehrte/r... , and Mit freundlichen Gr√º√üen
    • Informal: Liebe/r... , and Viele Gr√º√üe or Liebe Gr√º√üe
  • Start with a lowercase letter: After the salutation and comma (e.g., Liebe Anna,), the first word of the email starts with a lowercase letter (e.g., ich schreibe dir...).
  • Keep it simple: Use simple sentences and vocabulary you are confident with. Don't try to write complex sentences. Correct and simple is better than complex and wrong.
  • Use conjunctions: Use simple words like und, aber, and denn to connect your ideas. For example: "Ich komme gern, aber ich bin ein bisschen sp√§t."

Part 3: Bitten & Verbote formulieren (Formulating Requests & Prohibitions)

In the final part of the oral exam, you will see a picture (e.g., a glass of water, a "no smoking" sign). Your task is to use the picture to form a request (`Bitte`) or state a prohibition (`Verbot`). Your partner will then react to what you said.

Essential Phrases & Grammar for Part 3

How to Formulate a Request (eine Bitte)

There are two main ways to make a request. Always remember to use "bitte" to be polite.

  • Using the Imperative (Command Form):

  • Using a Question with "k√∂nnen": (This is very polite and common)

    • Formal (Sie): K√∂nnen Sie mir bitte ... geben? (Can you please give me...?)

How to Formulate a Prohibition (ein Verbot)

The easiest and most common way is to use the modal verb "d√ºrfen".

  • Structure: Hier darf man nicht... (+ verb). For example: Hier darf man nicht rauchen. (One is not allowed to smoke here.)
  • Alternative: ... ist verboten. For example: Rauchen ist hier verboten. (Smoking is forbidden here.)

How to React (Reagieren)

  • Reacting positively to a request: Ja, nat√ºrlich. / Gerne. / Klar. / Hier, bitte. (Yes, of course. / Gladly. / Sure. / Here you go.)
